Two Layer QG Model Example¶

Here is a quick overview of how to use the two-layer model. See the pyqg.QGModel api documentation for further details.

First import numpy, matplotlib, and pyqg:

In [1]: import numpy as np

In [2]: from matplotlib import pyplot as plt

In [3]: import pyqg

Initialize and Run the Model¶

Here we set up a model which will run for 10 years and start averaging after 5 years. There are lots of parameters that can be specified as keyword arguments but we are just using the defaults.

In [4]: year = 24*60*60*360.

In [5]: m = pyqg.QGModel(tmax=10*year, twrite=10000, tavestart=5*year)

In [6]: m.run()
t=        72000000, tc=     10000: cfl=0.097273, ke=0.000490861
t=       144000000, tc=     20000: cfl=0.101345, ke=0.000493633
t=       216000000, tc=     30000: cfl=0.078750, ke=0.000437760
t=       288000000, tc=     40000: cfl=0.086608, ke=0.000459623

Visualize Output¶

We access the actual pv values through the attribute m.q. The first axis of q corresponds with the layer number. (Remeber that in python, numbering starts at 0.)

In [7]: q_upper = m.q[0] + m.Qy[0]*m.y

In [8]: plt.contourf(m.x, m.y, q_upper, 12, cmap='RdBu_r')
Out[8]: <matplotlib.contour.QuadContourSet instance at 0x108c51320>

In [9]: plt.xlabel('x'); plt.ylabel('y'); plt.title('Upper Layer PV')
Out[9]: <matplotlib.text.Text at 0x109189950>

In [10]: plt.colorbar();

Plot Diagnostics¶

The model automatically accumulates averages of certain diagnostics. We can find out what diagnostics are available by calling

In [11]: m.describe_diagnostics()
NAME       | DESCRIPTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
APEflux    | spectral flux of available potential energy           
APEgen     | total APE generation                                  
APEgenspec | spectrum of APE generation                            
EKE        | mean eddy kinetic energy                              
EKEdiss    | total energy dissipation by bottom drag               
Ensspec    | enstrophy spectrum                                    
KEflux     | spectral flux of kinetic energy                       
KEspec     |  kinetic energy spectrum                              
entspec    | barotropic enstrophy spectrum                         
q          | QGPV

To look at the wavenumber energy spectrum, we plot the KEspec diagnostic. (Note that summing along the l-axis, as in this example, does not give us a true isotropic wavenumber spectrum.)

In [12]: kespec_u = m.get_diagnostic('KEspec')[0].sum(axis=0)

In [13]: kespec_l = m.get_diagnostic('KEspec')[1].sum(axis=0)

In [14]: plt.loglog( m.kk, kespec_u, '.-' )
Out[14]: [<matplotlib.lines.Line2D at 0x109480450>]

In [15]: plt.loglog( m.kk, kespec_l, '.-' )
Out[15]: [<matplotlib.lines.Line2D at 0x1094e6bd0>]

In [16]: plt.legend(['upper layer','lower layer'], loc='lower left')
Out[16]: <matplotlib.legend.Legend at 0x109480e10>

In [17]: plt.ylim([1e-9,1e-3]); plt.xlim([m.kk.min(), m.kk.max()])
Out[17]: (6.2831853071795867e-06, 0.00020106192982974677)

In [18]: plt.xlabel(r'k (m$^{-1}$)'); plt.grid()

In [19]: plt.title('Kinetic Energy Spectrum')
Out[19]: <matplotlib.text.Text at 0x109505690>

We can also plot the spectral fluxes of energy.

In [20]: ebud = [ -m.get_diagnostic('APEgenspec').sum(axis=0),
   ....:          -m.get_diagnostic('APEflux').sum(axis=0),
   ....:          -m.get_diagnostic('KEflux').sum(axis=0),
   ....:          -m.rek*m.del2*m.get_diagnostic('KEspec')[1].sum(axis=0)*m.M**2 ]
   ....: 

In [21]: ebud.append(-np.vstack(ebud).sum(axis=0))

In [22]: ebud_labels = ['APE gen','APE flux','KE flux','Diss.','Resid.']

In [23]: [plt.semilogx(m.kk, term) for term in ebud]

In [24]: plt.legend(ebud_labels, loc='upper right')
